# Design Philosophy Style Library: 20 Systems

> A design style library for visual design (web / PPT / PDF / infographics / illustration / apps, etc.).
> Each style provides: philosophical core + key traits + **what the studio actually ships** + prompt DNA (used in combination with scene templates).

## How to read each entry

- **Philosophy** — the studio's stated thesis, the thing they would say if asked.
- **Key traits** — surface-level visual signals.
- **What [Studio] actually ships** — their professional substrate. *What medium, what technique, what kind of artifact.* This is the anchor against which any "in the style of" attempt should be measured.
- **Prompt DNA** — the recipe for combination with scene templates.

### How to read this table

> **Star columns.** Does this style work as this medium? ★★★ = the medium IS the substrate (the school actually ships this) · ★★☆ = adapts competently to this medium · ★☆☆ = substrate fights this medium, or the school doesn't ship this at all.

> **Editorial Web vs Experiential Web.** Web is two different jobs. *Editorial Web* = page-oriented sites: landing pages, content, marketing, portfolios, editorial documents. The page IS the artifact. *Experiential Web* = experience-oriented sites: WebGL showcases, scroll-cinema, lobby-gated brand pieces, live interactive canvas. The experience IS the artifact. Most schools score ★★★ in one and ★☆☆ in the other — they pick a substrate. A school strong at both is rare; a school weak at both (19 Boom, 15 Thorp) means web in either form isn't their medium.

> **PowerPoint vs HTML5 Slides.** Slides are two different jobs too. *PowerPoint* (.pptx via `html2pptx.js` converter) = static frames, no JavaScript, no canvas, no WebGL. CSS gradients forbidden, animation reduced to first-frame-only, custom SVG rasterized to PNG. The four hard constraints from `export-formats.md` define what survives. Schools whose substrate is anti-motion + typography + solids (09 Jetset is the perfect case) score ★★★. Schools whose substrate IS the live execution (06 Active Theory, 07 Field.io, 08 Resn, 13 Lieberman, 14 Kwok, 20 Neo Shen) die on conversion — frozen first-frames are cosplay, not their work. *HTML5 Slides* = browser-rendered click-through decks (reveal.js / deck-stage). Full JavaScript, canvas, WebGL — the live substrate is preserved. The opposite of PowerPoint: schools whose substrate dies in PPTX often thrive in HTML5 Slides. 09 Jetset is the inverse case here — anti-motion as published position fights HTML5's interactive medium → ★☆☆.

> **Where it's strong / Where it's weak.** Per-row prose carrying the prerequisite gate. The strength names what the school requires to land — including, where it matters, the production method ("HTML", "AI image, never HTML"). The weakness names the brief conditions that make this school the wrong reach. **Read these two cells before you commit to a style.** The stars tell you the medium will support it; the prose tells you whether your brief actually qualifies.

### The two rules this table is gated by

> **Cosplay rule.** A studio's "look" is downstream of their substrate. Copying fonts and palette without copying substrate (medium, technique, artifact-class) produces cosplay.

> **Problem-fit rule.** A school is for the problem it was built to solve. Most of these twenty schools were built for hard problems with prerequisites — Active Theory needs immersion-as-positioning, Information Architects needs 4–6 parallel content streams, Field.io needs procedural-art-as-substrate, Territory Studio needs film context. Reach for a school because of its surface distinctiveness, without the prerequisite it requires, and what ships is the studio's signature applied to a problem that doesn't need it (Territory on a café page, Active Theory on a portfolio, IA's multi-stream grid forced onto single-thread content). The substrate refuses to ship without its prerequisite. What lands in its place is decoration.

### The simple-problem reach list

When the brief is single-thread content — one business, one product, one café, one service page, one portfolio — with no spatial data, no live input, no scientific data, no film context, and no immersive pretension, the schools whose prerequisites you can actually meet are these:

- **01 Pentagram** — if the page can carry one bold typographic gesture (a wordmark, a stat, a single hero crop) and stay quietly disciplined elsewhere.
- **11 Build** — if the brand earns print-grade editorial restraint (high-end product / brand with material rigor).
- **18 Kenya Hara** — if the brand is comfortable being Muji-grade quiet (most small businesses are not — they need to assert presence).
- **10 Müller-Brockmann** — only if the page genuinely benefits from grid discipline (timetables, reference, dense menus). Not for atmospheric mood.
- **02 Stamen Design** — only if a place is genuinely the subject (a destination, a route, a spatial claim).
- **17 Takram** — only if there's actual research depth and cross-domain framing.
- **20 Neo Shen** — only if atmospheric mood is the subject (poetic / classical / contemplative brand).

The other thirteen schools were built for harder problems — immersive showcases, multi-stream newspapers, film FUI, scientific journals, generative-art-as-identity, real-time-input drawing, artist books. They will not land on a small landing page. Their prerequisite isn't there. What ships is their surface signature on the wrong problem.

## I. Information Architecture School (01–04)
> Philosophy: "Data is not decoration, it is building material"

### 01. Pentagram — Michael Bierut style
**Philosophy**: type is language, the grid is thought
**Key traits**:
- Extremely restrained color (black/white + 1 brand color)
- Modern interpretation of the Swiss grid system
- Typography as primary visual language
- Strategic use of negative space (60%+ whitespace)

**What Pentagram actually ships**:
Pentagram is a 50+ year partnership (London / NYC / Berlin / Austin), Michael Bierut + Paula Scher + Natasha Jen + Eddie Opara + ~24 partners. Their substrate is **editorial typography systems for institutions** — Hillary Clinton 2016, MIT Media Lab, the New York Times typography refresh, Mastercard, Citibank. They ship identity systems and editorial documents. They do **not** ship motion-design-as-spectacle. The typical Pentagram move is *one bold typographic gesture* (a custom wordmark, a single hero crop, a structural grid that earns the page) — quietly disciplined elsewhere. Their websites are editorial documents, not interactive showcases.

**Prompt DNA**:
```
Pentagram/Michael Bierut style:
- Extreme typographic hierarchy, Helvetica/Univers family
- Swiss grid with precise mathematical spacing
- Black/white + one accent color (#HEX)
- Information architecture as visual structure
- 60%+ whitespace ratio
- Data visualization as primary decoration
- One bold typographic gesture per composition (a stat, a wordmark, a single hero crop)
- Animation, if any, is one scroll-shadow on the header — never spectacle
```

**Signature work**: Hillary Clinton 2016 campaign identity
**Search keywords**: pentagram hillary logo system

### 02. Stamen Design — Data poetics
**Philosophy**: turn data into a landscape you can touch
**Key traits**:
- Cartographic thinking applied to information design
- Algorithm-generated organic graphics
- Warm data-visualization palette (terracotta, sage green, deep blue)
- Interactive layered hierarchy

**What Stamen actually ships**:
Cartography studio (San Francisco, founded 2001 by Eric Rodenbeck — partly absorbed into Stadia Maps after 2023). Their substrate is **maps that are maps** — watercolor map tiles served as TMS / WMS layers, custom OpenStreetMap renderings, terrain-tile servers, data-driven cartographic narrative. They ship the rigour of cartographic convention (proper contour lines, fault-line hatching, compass roses with declination, scale bars, graticules) plus modern typography (IBM Plex Mono in margins, Fraunces or similar serif for titles), terracotta + sage + deep-blue palette, contour lines as the primary visual primitive. **A Stamen page is a map**, or it is an essay about a map.

**Prompt DNA**:
```
Stamen Design aesthetic:
- Cartographic approach to data visualization
- Real spatial/geographic data, not decorative shapes
- Contour lines, fault-line hatching, compass roses, graticules — cartographic conventions
- Warm palette (terracotta, sage green, deep blues)
- Layered information like topographic maps
- Hand-crafted feel despite digital precision
- Soft shadows and depth
- IBM Plex Mono for marginalia, Fraunces / serif for titles
- If interactive, the interaction is spatial: pan, zoom, time-slider, layer-toggle
```

**Signature work**: COVID-19 surge map (motion-aware), watercolor map tile server
**Search keywords**: stamen covid map visualization, stamen watercolor tiles

### 03. Information Architects — Information chunked into viewport-appropriate reading lanes

**Founder / lineage**: Oliver Reichenstein, Zürich / Tokyo (founded 2005). Best known for the iA Writer text editor and for redesigning the websites of NZZ, Süddeutsche Zeitung, Die Welt, Tages-Anzeiger, and the Russian newspaper Vedomosti.

**The thesis (Reichenstein, 2006)**: *"Web Design is 95% Typography."* What designers call "layout" is in truth a stack of unseen typographic decisions — measure, leading, color, scale, vertical rhythm. Get those four right and the page composes itself. Decoration applied on top of broken typography only hides the breakage.

**Philosophy — the actual principle**: Information Architecture is the discipline of **chunking content into the right number of parallel reading lanes for the viewport you have.** A 1920×1080 desktop has horizontal real estate for 4–6 simultaneous content streams. A 768px tablet has room for 2–3. A 375px phone has room for 1. The same content reorganizes at each breakpoint — not just resizes. The page is read like a newspaper: the reader picks an entry point, scans across, dives in. Hierarchy is carried by typography and grid placement, not by boxes, buttons, shadows, or backgrounds. Density is a virtue. Empty horizontal space is failure to use the medium.

**The four typographic axes IA tunes**:
1. **Measure** — line length. ~66 characters per line is optimal **for any single prose stream.** This rule applies PER PROSE BLOCK, not to the page. A 1920px page can have three parallel 66ch prose columns, or two prose columns plus a marginalia rail plus a sidebar — all simultaneously, all readable.
2. **Leading** — line height. ~1.55–1.65 for body. Tighter for headlines, looser for small print.
3. **Color** — the visual weight of text on the page (a function of size × weight × tracking × density). Pages should feel balanced across all reading lanes, not striped.
4. **Scale** — modular ratio between sizes. 1.25 (major third), 1.333 (perfect fourth), or 1.618 (golden) — explicit and measurable.

**The cardinal misreading (do not commit this)**: "IA = narrow centered column on white." That confuses the **iA Writer app aesthetic** (single content stream, single column) with the **IA Studio system** (heterogeneous content, multi-column newspaper grid). iA Writer is a writing tool — one stream of prose, one column is correct. **A landing page, a café page, a practice page, a product page is NOT a single content stream.** It contains: hero, navigation, biographies, lists, tables, image galleries, contact, footer. Each is a different content type. Each gets its own grid placement and treatment. **Forcing all of that into a 720px centered column is the opposite of IA — it is laziness mistaken for restraint.** A 1920px viewport with 1180px of empty grey on either side is design failure, not minimalism.

**The newspaper analogy (Reichenstein's actual practice)**: NZZ.ch on a 1920 viewport is laid out as a 12-column macro grid with multiple parallel content streams — a hero story occupies cols 1-7, a side rail of related briefs cols 8-12, a band of teasers below splits 4+4+4, a stats block runs 3+3+3+3. The reader's eye chooses where to enter. The same page on a tablet (1024) collapses to 2-3 streams. On mobile (375) it stacks to 1. **The content rearranges, never just shrinks.**

**What IA Studio actually ships**:
The iA Writer Mac/iOS/Windows/Android app is one substrate (single-stream prose editor). The newspaper redesigns are the other (multi-stream grids). Variable-font usage with optical sizing axis (`font-optical-sizing: auto` on Newsreader, IBM Plex, or Tiempos) is a current IA Studio hallmark. Drop-cap on the lede only, classic blue/visited-purple link colors preserved (not stripped to "design system neutral"), §-numbered band sections, JetBrains Mono / SF Mono for marginalia, real `<table>` elements with `<caption>`, `<thead>`, `<tbody>`, `tabular-nums`, real `<dl>`/`<dt>`/`<dd>` for definition lists.

**Prompt DNA**:
```
Information Architects (Reichenstein) philosophy:
- Information chunked into parallel reading lanes — viewport-appropriate
- 1920+ uses the WHOLE WIDTH with 4-6 simultaneous content streams
- 12-column macro grid as chassis; sections place themselves on cols
- Newspaper pattern: hero spans cols 1-9, side rail cols 10-12;
  next band biographies 4+4+4; residents 6-up; menu 4+4+4; etc.
- Each section is a 12-col band with §-number in mono and 1px top rule
- Multi-column prose where appropriate (column-count: 2-3) — 66ch each
- Marginalia in mono is a parallel semantic layer (right column on wide,
  interleaved blocks on mobile) — facts, mini-tables, footnotes
- Modular type scale with explicit ratio (1.25 / 1.333 / 1.618)
- Charter / Source Serif / Newsreader body, JetBrains Mono / SF Mono marginalia
- Newsreader with font-optical-sizing: auto (opsz axis 6..72) is the variable-font hallmark
- Classic blue (#0000EE) and purple (#551A8B) hyperlinks
- Drop cap on lede only, in accent color (oxblood / terracotta)
- Italic H2s, hierarchy via italic + scale + spacing
- Data tables are real tables (caption, thead, tbody, tabular-nums)
- Menus / specs are dl/dt/dd
- Hero figures full-bleed at clamp(360px, 64vh, 760px); never 720px stamps
- Breakpoint logic: 1920+ multi-lane, 1024 collapse to 2-3 lanes,
  768 collapse to 6-col, 375 stack to 1 column.
  Content REARRANGES at each breakpoint, never just shrinks.
- Information dense across the WHOLE viewport — no empty horizontal grey.
```

**Signature works**: iA Writer (app — single-stream context). NZZ.ch redesign, Süddeutsche Zeitung redesign, Die Welt redesign, Vedomosti, the IA blog itself (ia.net) — all multi-stream contexts.

**Reading**: Reichenstein, *"Web Design is 95% Typography"* (2006). Bringhurst, *The Elements of Typographic Style* (canonical source for measure and modular scale). Tschichold, *Asymmetric Typography*. Tufte, *Envisioning Information* (marginalia logic). Look at the actual NZZ.ch or sueddeutsche.de homepages on a wide viewport to see the school in production.

**Search keywords**: information architects, ia.net, oliver reichenstein, ia writer, web design 95 typography, NZZ redesign, sueddeutsche redesign, marginalia web layout, modular type scale, 12-column newspaper grid, multi-stream reading layout

### 04. Fathom Information Design — Scientific narrative
**Philosophy**: every pixel must carry information
**Key traits**:
- Rigor of a scientific journal + design elegance
- Precise visualization of quantitative data
- Calm professional palette (gray, navy)
- Annotation and citation systems treated as design elements

**What Fathom actually ships**:
Two things share the name. **Fathom Information Design** (Boston, founded 2010 by Ben Fry, ex-Processing co-creator) ships scientific-journal-grade data visualization for the Gates Foundation, Nike, GE, and clients who need real quantitative narrative. Their substrate is *one number on the page that matters*, framed by footnote-grade citation hygiene and tabular precision. The other Fathom is **Fathom Analytics** (privacy-first analytics dashboard, electric-cyan brand) — different studio, different palette. Most "Fathom-style" prompts mean Fry's Fathom Information Design. They ship in HTML and PDF, never in motion. Their charts are *the joke*: when a single flat line tells the whole story, that flat line *is* the visualization.

**Prompt DNA**:
```
Fathom Information Design style:
- Scientific journal aesthetic meets modern design
- Precise data visualization (charts, timelines, scatter plots)
- Neutral scheme (grays, navy, one highlight color)
- Footnote/citation design integrated into layout
- Clean sans-serif (GT America/Graphik)
- Information density without clutter
- One dominating number per spread (the headline number)
- Tabular nums on every numeric value
- Real <sup> footnotes with corresponding <small> blocks
- No chartjunk — every line on the chart encodes data
- Static — read like a scientific paper, not animated
```

**Signature work**: Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation annual report
**Search keywords**: fathom information design gates foundation, ben fry fathom

## II. Motion Poetics School (05–08)
> Philosophy: "Technology itself is a flowing poem"

### 05. Locomotive — Scroll-narrative master
**Philosophy**: scrolling is not browsing, it is a journey
**Key traits**:
- Silky parallax scrolling
- Cinematic shot-by-shot narrative
- Bold spatial whitespace
- Precise choreography of dynamic elements

**What Locomotive actually ships**:
Locomotive Studio (Montréal, founded 2014). Authors of `locomotive-scroll`, the scroll-coupled animation library that powers most of the Awwwards-grade smooth-scroll category. Substrate: **scroll position as primary input**. Pin sections, horizontally-scrolled inner tracks, parallax with damping. Their default move: vertical scroll triggers cinema. They ship dark themes with Bebas Neue / Anton display + a tiny mono. The library default damping is 0.1 — without damping, scroll-coupled motion feels mechanical. Locomotive sites typically use Lenis (the successor library, also from the same lineage) or GSAP ScrollTrigger.

**Prompt DNA**:
```
Locomotive scroll narrative style:
- Film-like scene composition with parallax depth
- Generous vertical spacing between sections
- Bold typography emerging from darkness
- Smooth motion blur effects
- Dark mode (near-black backgrounds)
- Strategic glowing accents
- Hero sections 100vh tall
- Scroll-coupled transforms with damping (lerp 0.1 default)
- Horizontal-scroll-from-vertical-scroll on at least one section
- Section pinning where the camera holds while content advances
```

**Signature work**: Lusion.co website
**Search keywords**: locomotive scroll lusion, locomotive-scroll library, lenis smooth scroll

### 06. Active Theory — WebGL poet
**Philosophy**: making technology visible is making technology comprehensible
**Key traits**:
- 3D particle systems as core element
- Real-time-rendered data visualization
- Mouse-interaction-driven world building
- Neon and deep-space palette

**What Active Theory actually ships**:
Active Theory (Venice Beach, LA, founded 2010). Immersive web for Google, Tesla, Beats, X-Men franchises, the WebGL Olympics. Their substrate is **WebGL + canvas heavy with cross-system motion design coordination**. Glass-with-depth panels, 3D tilt on cursor, particle fields, scroll-coupled scene composition, ambient HUD chrome (live clocks, uptime counters, environmental data fetched live). Aesthetic is military-tech-future: monospace, grid overlays, scanline glow, cinematic interaction grammar. Their sites typically have a *lobby gate* (state-machine entry persisted in localStorage) before the experience begins. The "live HUD chrome" — Asia/Riyadh clocks, uptime tickers, weather APIs — is the substrate's DNA, not decoration.

**Prompt DNA**:
```
Active Theory WebGL aesthetic:
- Particle systems representing data flow (real rAF tick, not single-frame)
- 3D visualization in depth space
- Neon gradients (cyan/magenta/electric blue) on dark
- Mouse-reactive environment — cursor moves panels, particles, numerals
- Depth of field and bokeh effects
- Floating UI with glassmorphism
- Live HUD chrome: clock, uptime, env data
- Lobby-gate state machine persisted in localStorage
- Section-enter SFX choreography (debounced, scroll-position-aware)
- IntersectionObserver-triggered reveals with multi-card stagger (~280ms between cards)
```

**Signature work**: NASA Prospect
**Search keywords**: active theory nasa webgl

### 07. Field.io — Algorithmic aesthetic
**Philosophy**: code is the designer
**Key traits**:
- Generative art system
- Dynamic graphics that differ on every visit
- Intelligent choreography of abstract geometry
- Balance of technical and artistic

**What Field.io actually ships**:
Field (London / Berlin, founded 2008 by Marcus Wendt and Vera-Maria Glahn). Generative design studio working with Aphex Twin (the famous "Collapse" EP visuals), Vera Molnár tributes, Adobe MAX work, the Stripe brand systems. Their substrate is **procedural systems** — Voronoi/Delaunay tessellations, generative type, audio-reactive visuals, deterministic randomness via seeded RNG (mulberry32 is their go-to). The aesthetic is rigorous-geometric, monochromatic, with single-color accent. **Variable-font axis manipulation as primary expressive primitive** — they use the wght/wdth/opsz axes the way other studios use color. They publish papers and blog posts about font-axis sweeps as design material.

**Prompt DNA**:
```
Field.io generative design style:
- Abstract geometric patterns, algorithmically generated
- Dynamic composition that feels computational
- Monochromatic base with vibrant accent
- Mathematical precision in spacing
- Voronoi diagrams or Delaunay triangulation with documented seed
- Variable-font axis as primary expressive primitive
  (wght, wdth, opsz — sweep them on scroll/hover/time)
- Seeded RNG (mulberry32) for deterministic randomness
- Clean code aesthetic
- If audio-reactive: real AudioContext + AnalyserNode + rAF analyser loop
- Reduced-motion fallback to ambient sine wave so visual still moves when muted
```

**Signature work**: British Council digital installations, Aphex Twin Collapse visuals
**Search keywords**: field.io generative design, field marcus wendt

## III. Minimalist School (09–12)
> Philosophy: "Cut until you cannot cut anymore"

### 09. Experimental Jetset — Conceptual minimalism
**Philosophy**: one idea = one form
**Key traits**:
- A single visual metaphor running through the entire design
- Mondrian palette of blue/red/yellow + black/white
- Typography as graphic
- Anti-commercial honest design

**What Experimental Jetset actually ships**:
EJ (Amsterdam, founded 1997, three partners: Marieke Stolk, Erwin Brinkers, Danny van den Dungen). **Anti-motion modernist purists.** Whitney Museum identity (the famous "responsive W"), MoMA print work, Princess Tam Tam, Henrik Vibskov collaborations, identity for the Stedelijk Museum. **Their published position: they don't believe in interaction.** Their websites are Helvetica-grid documents that refuse to move. They've written manifestos about it. Their substrate is the *refusal* to ship motion.

**Prompt DNA**:

### 10. Müller-Brockmann lineage — Swiss-grid purism
**Philosophy**: objectivity is beauty
**Key traits**:
- Mathematically precise grid system (8pt baseline)
- Absolute left alignment or centering
- Single- or two-color schemes
- Functionalism above all

**What Brockmann actually ships(/shipped)**:
Josef Müller-Brockmann (1914–1996, Zurich). The grid bible (*Grid Systems in Graphic Design*, 1981). The aesthetic isn't grid-as-decoration; it's grid-as-information-architecture. Type set at 8pt on 12pt baseline; hierarchy expressed through scale, weight, and column position only. **Prompt DNA**:
```
Josef Müller-Brockmann Swiss modernism:

- Two-color maximum (black + one accent)
- Akzidenz-Grotesk or similar rationalist typeface
- No decorative elements
- Timeless, objective aesthetic
- Grid must serve information density, not decoration
- Static — Brockmann's substrate is print, transposed
```

## How to Use the Prompts

**Combination formula**: `[style prompt DNA] + [scene template (see scene-templates.md)] + [specific content]`

### Core principle: describe mood, not layout (Mood, Not Layout)

The key to AI image generation: short prompts > long prompts. Three sentences describing mood and content beat 30 lines of layout detail.

| Diversity-killing writing | Creativity-firing writing |
|----------------|----------------|
| Specifying color ratios (60%/25%/15%) | Describing mood ("warm like Sunday morning") |
| Prescribing layout positions ("title centered, image on right") | Citing a specific aesthetic ("Pentagram editorial feel") |
| Constraining character poses and expressions | Letting the AI interpret the style naturally |
| Listing every visual element to include | Describing what the audience should feel |

### Good / Bad examples

**Bad — over-constrained (AI output is empty and flat):**
```
Professional presentation slide. Dark background, light text.
Title centered at top. Two columns below. Left column: bullet points.
Right column: bar chart. Colors: navy 60%, white 30%, gold 10%.
Font size: title 36pt, body 18pt. Margins: 40px all sides.
```

**Good — mood-driven (varied, textured output):**
```
A data visualization that feels like a Bloomberg Businessweek
editorial spread. The key number "28.5%" should dominate the
composition like a headline. Warm cream tones with sharp black
typography. The data tells a story of dramatic channel shift.
```

### Choosing how to produce it

Production method is implicit in the "Where it's strong" cell of the Style × Medium table. Read it there. The shorthand:

- **HTML rendering** — styles that depend on precise typography and data (01 Pentagram, 03 IA, 04 Fathom, 10 Brockmann, 11 Build, 17 Takram, 18 Hara). Code controls data and layout.
- **AI generation** — styles with explicit visual elements (06 Active Theory, 07 Field.io, 12 Sagmeister, 13 Lieberman, 14 Kwok, 15 Thorp, 16 Territory, 20 Neo Shen). Image generated straight out via Nano Banana / Midjourney.
- **Hybrid** — HTML skeleton layout + AI generates imagery / backgrounds (02 Stamen, 05 Locomotive, 08 Resn, 09 Jetset, 19 Boom).
- **Video embedded** — for substrates whose artifact-class is film (15 Thorp, 16 Territory when not pulling back to FUI canvas).

Pick the production method only AFTER you've passed the problem-fit gate (the brief matches the row's "Where it's strong" and does NOT match its "Where it's weak").

### Quality control

1. ❌ Don't just write "in the style of Pentagram" → ✅ describe with concrete design traits
2. Text frequently breaks in AI output → replace text after generation
3. Proportions easily distort → explicitly specify aspect ratio
4. Generate 3–5 variations first, refine after picking the best

**Default aesthetic no-go zones** (the user can override per their brand):
- ❌ Cyber-neon / dark blue background (#0D1117)
- ❌ Personal signature/watermark on cover images

### The substrate-authenticity test (one-line summary per cluster)

Before claiming any of the 20 voices, ask one question:

| Cluster | The one question |
|---|---|
| Information Architecture (01–04) | Does the typography do the work, with no decoration on top? |
| Motion Poetics (05–08) | **Does it actually move?** rAF, canvas, scroll-coupled, WebGL — the substrate is verifiable. |
| Minimalist (09–12) | Does it have the discipline to refuse what it's not? (Or for Sagmeister Walsh: is there a photographed physical artifact?) |
| Avant-garde (13–16) | Is the process visible? (Lieberman: drawing live. Kwok: recursing live. Thorp: rendered cinema. Territory: continuous FUI motion.) |
| Eastern (17–20) | Does the work disappear into purpose? (Or for Boom: is the artifact-class right?) |

If you can't answer YES to the cluster's question, you're not in the voice — you have the studio's name on a generic template.
