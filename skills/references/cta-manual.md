# How to Construct a Great CTA

## An instruction manual for any narrative artifact — landing pages, booking flows, slide decks, scrollytelling pages, dashboards, personal sites.

---

## THE PRINCIPLE

A great CTA doesn't describe an action. It creates a feeling that makes the action feel inevitable.

The reader / viewer / audience should not think "I should click this." They should feel something — guilt, warmth, urgency, pride, recognition — that makes the action a reflex, not a decision.

This applies whether the artifact is a 12-slide investor pitch, a scrollytelling case study, a real-time dashboard, or a single landing page.

---

## THE STRUCTURE RULE

Every narrative artifact has (at least) two CTA positions. They do different jobs.

**OPENING CTA (top of artifact — top of page, cover slide, opening chapter, dashboard hero panel):** Functional. Low-friction. Wayfinding. The reader knows nothing yet. They haven't earned context and you haven't earned trust. The opening CTA says "enter" or "scroll" or "start" or "begin." It is deliberately boring. Emotion here is a stranger grabbing your arm on the street. It's cheap, unearned, and it repels.

**CLOSING CTA (bottom of artifact — bottom of page, last slide, last chapter, dashboard footer):** Emotional. Earned. Specific. By now the reader has scrolled / advanced / explored everything — the story, the proof, the characters, the data. They *know* this place or this person or this company. The closing CTA is the payoff. It uses what the artifact already established to create a feeling the reader can't ignore.

**The rule:** The opening CTA is a door. The closing CTA is a destination. Never reverse them.

### Format-specific mapping

| Format | Opening CTA position | Closing CTA position |
|---|---|---|
| Landing page | Hero section button | Footer / final section button |
| Slide deck | Cover slide CTA | Last slide CTA |
| Scrollytelling | First sticky chapter | Last sticky chapter |
| Dashboard | KPI hero panel button | Drill-down summary button |
| 3D / Product Demo | Initial scene CTA | Post-rotation final scene CTA |
| Timeline | Phase 1 entry button | Final phase / completion button |
| Interactive module | Module entry button | Module completion / next-module button |

## THE EARNING MECHANISM

The closing CTA can only land if the artifact earns it. Here's what "earning" means:

The CTA is a **callback**, not a setup. It references things the reader already encountered. If the artifact never introduced grandma, you can't invoke grandma. If the deck never mentioned a 47-0 winning streak, you can't use "be there for 48." If the dashboard never showed a quarterly trend break, you can't end with "approve the pivot."

Think of it like a punchline. The joke only works if the setup came first. A closing CTA that references something the artifact didn't build is a punchline to a joke nobody heard.

**How an artifact earns its CTA — the FalCaMel example:**

The page for a fictional Saudi cat café on a cliff (FalCaMel) follows this arc:

| Section | What it earns |
|---------|--------------|
| Hero: "Grandma's House. On the Edge." | Establishes the grandma metaphor |
| The Lounge: "Grandma's living room. 200 meters above everything you thought mattered." | Builds the world — you're inside her house now |
| The Residents: Sultan the falcon (47-0 vs. the rollercoaster), Haboob the camel (judges your outfit), Shams and Qamar the rescue cats | You've met the family |
| Our Majlis: "She'll make you feel like the only guest in the world." | Warmth deepened |
| What Grandma Made: luqaimat, kunafa, Jareen single-origin qahwa, mutabbaq. "You're not ordering. You're accepting what's offered." "Grandma-sized portions. Yes, all of it." | She's been cooking. You can smell it. |
| The Full FalCaMel: evening programs, SAR 2,000 experiences | The full picture |
| Find Us: "If you get lost, look up. We're on the edge." | You know where she lives |

**Then the footer lands:**

> "Grandma's house. 200 meters up. She made too much food again. She always does. Don't keep her waiting."

Every word in that CTA was earned by the page. "Too much food" — you saw the menu. "Don't keep her waiting" — you've been in her house for five minutes. The guilt is real because the generosity was real. The warmth is real because you met her residents and ate her food.

The same arc applies to a slide deck, a scrollytelling case study, a dashboard, or a video. Build the world / make the case / show the data → THEN cash the CTA.

## THE BENCHMARK

Here are the opening and closing CTAs from five different vibes of the same café. Study the contrast:

| Vibe | Opening CTA (top — boring) | Closing CTA (bottom — earned) |
|------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Grandmother's House** | "Find Your Seat" | "Grandma's Not Getting Any Younger." / *She made luqaimat. She made kunafa. She's been cooking since you said you were coming. The coffee's already poured.* / **[I'm Coming, I'm Coming]** |
| **Qahwa Tales** | "Taste the Altitude" | *(narrative closes with the Royal FalCaMel — the complete story earns the booking)* |
| **The Race** | "Watch The Race" | "THE STREAK CONTINUES" / *47 races. 47 wins. Be there for 48.* / **[Witness the Streak]** |
| **The Antidote** | *(no opening CTA — the page just pulls you in)* | "Your seat is waiting." / *No rush. It will still be here.* / **[Find Your Seat]** |
| **Majlis** | "Claim Your Sanctuary" | "Your Throne Awaits" / *The Majlis opens its doors to precious few. Will you be among the chosen?* / **[Request Royal Audience]** |

Notice: every opening CTA could belong to any business. "Find Your Seat." "Taste the Altitude." Generic. Functional. That's correct — the top hasn't earned specificity yet.

Every closing CTA could only belong to *this* business. "I'm Coming, I'm Coming" only works after grandma. "Be there for 48" only works after you've met Sultan. "No rush. It will still be here." only works after a page that deliberately moved slowly. The closing CTA is the artifact's fingerprint.

## ANTI-PATTERNS: WHAT FAILS

**1. Generic FOMO**
"Only 3 spots left!" / "Limited availability — book now!" / "Last chance!"
This is fear, not feeling. It works on commodity products (hotel rooms, flights). It's death for anything with personality. It tells the reader you don't trust your own artifact to be compelling, so you're manufacturing urgency instead.

**2. Corporate action language**
"Schedule your consultation today" / "Get started" / "Learn more" / "Contact us" / "Request a demo"
This describes a button function. It tells the reader what happens when they click, which they already know. It creates zero feeling. It's the equivalent of a waiter saying "this is a fork" before you eat.

**3. Unearned emotion at the top**
"Your life is about to change!" / "Join the family!" / "This is where it all begins!"
Emotional language before the artifact has established anything. The reader hasn't met the family. They don't know what "it" is. This is the stranger grabbing your arm. It doesn't create warmth — it creates suspicion.

**4. Clever-for-clever's-sake**
"Don't just stand there. Sit down." / "You know you want to." / "What are you waiting for?"
These try to be witty but they're generic. They could go on any page or slide for any business. A great closing CTA is specific to *this* artifact's content. If you can move the CTA to another deliverable and it still works, it's not good enough.

**5. The Wikipedia hedge**
"If you're interested in learning more about our offerings, feel free to reach out at your convenience."
This is compliance-officer mode. It hedges every possible commitment. It's the opposite of a reflex — it's an invitation to think about it later, which means never.

**6. Investor-deck tell-not-show (presentation-specific)**
Closing slide reads "We're raising $5M. Email us at hello@startup.com." After 15 slides of problem / solution / traction / team. The deck did the work; the closing CTA threw it away. Same anti-pattern as #2 but particularly common in pitch decks because founders mistake "professional" for "boring."

The earned version: a closing slide that calls back the strongest moment in the deck — the customer quote on slide 8, the chart on slide 11, the team's first principle on slide 4 — and turns it into the ask. "Sultan won 47 in a row. We're betting on the streak. Join us." beats "Series A: $5M. Contact founder@."

## HOW TO BUILD A CLOSING CTA: THE PROCESS

1. **Write / build the full artifact first.** The CTA comes last — literally last in your workflow. You cannot write a closing CTA for content that doesn't exist yet.

2. **List what the artifact earned.** What characters did the reader meet? What specific details did they absorb? What feelings did the artifact create — warmth, excitement, awe, trust, humor? For a deck: which slide hit hardest? For a dashboard: which number made them lean forward?

3. **Identify the gap.** What is the reader's situation right now (reading / advancing / scrolling, considering but not committed) versus where the business takes them (at the table, in the room, on the call, signing the term sheet)? The CTA lives in that gap.

4. **Write the CTA as a callback.** Use the artifact's own language, characters, imagery, or data points. The CTA should feel like the artifact's last line, not an add-on.

5. **Make the button text a human response, not an action label.** "I'm Coming, I'm Coming" is what you say to grandma. "Witness the Streak" is what you'd tell a friend. "Request Royal Audience" is how you'd describe it to someone. "Join the Streak" is what an investor types into a term-sheet email subject. The button is dialogue, not interface.

6. **Test it: reflex or decision?** Read / step through the full artifact, then read the CTA. Did you feel something, or did you evaluate something? If you evaluated, rewrite. A great CTA bypasses the rational brain.

## APPLYING THIS TO ANY ARTIFACT

This isn't just for cafés. The principle scales:

**Personal / career site:** The page walks through credentials, outcomes, career arc. The closing CTA should make the hiring manager feel the cost of *not* reaching out — not describe what happens if they do. The gap: they have a complex cross-border deal going sideways and no one in the room who reads the situation. The CTA lives in that gap.

**SaaS product:** The page walks through features, case studies, integrations. The closing CTA should invoke the specific pain the reader came with — not "Start your free trial" but something that makes them feel the weight of continuing without the product.

**Event / experience:** The page walks through what happens, who's there, what it feels like. The closing CTA should make absence feel like loss — not "Register now" but something that makes not-going feel like a missed chapter.

**Investor pitch deck:** The deck walks through problem → solution → traction → team → ask. The closing slide should call back the strongest moment in the deck — the customer quote, the trend chart, the founder's first principle — and turn it into the ask. "Series A: $5M" is action language. "Sultan won 47 in a row. Bet on the streak." is callback language.

**Sales / capabilities deck:** The deck walks through customer pain → our offering → proof → process. The closing CTA invokes the specific buyer pain established at the start. Don't end with "Get a demo." End with what the buyer's life looks like after they have what you sell.

**Conference talk:** The deck walks through narrative arc → insight → call. The closing slide isn't an URL or a Twitter handle. It's the thesis the talk earned, restated as something the audience now believes.

**Dashboard / status update:** The dashboard walks through metrics → trends → drill-downs. The closing footer / summary panel isn't "Approve / Reject." It's the decision the data already pointed to, named back at the viewer.

**Scrollytelling case study:** The page walks through situation → complication → resolution. The closing chapter doesn't say "Read more case studies." It cashes the resolution — what becomes possible, what changes, what's next.

**Educational / training module:** The module walks through concept → application → exercise. The closing CTA isn't "Continue to next module." It's the specific competency the learner just gained, named so they feel it.

**The universal test:** Can you move this CTA to a competitor's artifact and have it still work? If yes, it's generic. Kill it. A great closing CTA is load-bearing — it only stands because the artifact beneath it holds it up.

## SUMMARY FOR QUICK REFERENCE

| Position | Job | Emotion level | Specificity |
|----------|-----|--------------|-------------|
| Opening CTA (top of artifact) | Wayfinding | Zero — functional only | Generic is fine |
| Closing CTA (bottom of artifact) | Payoff | Maximum — earned by the artifact | Must be unique to this business and this artifact |

**The test:** Does the CTA describe an action, or trigger a reflex?

If it describes an action, it belongs at the top.
If it triggers a reflex, it belongs at the bottom.
If it does neither, delete it.
