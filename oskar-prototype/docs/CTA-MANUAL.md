# How to Construct a Great CTA

## An instruction manual for LLMs building landing pages, booking flows, and personal sites.

---

## THE PRINCIPLE

A great CTA doesn't describe an action. It creates a feeling that makes the action feel inevitable.

The reader should not think "I should click this." They should feel something — guilt, warmth, urgency, pride, recognition — that makes clicking a reflex, not a decision.

---

## THE STRUCTURE RULE

A page has (at least) two CTA positions. They do different jobs.

**HERO CTA (top of page):** Functional. Low-friction. Wayfinding. The reader knows nothing yet. They haven't earned context and you haven't earned trust. The hero CTA says "enter" or "scroll" or "start." It is deliberately boring. Emotion here is a stranger grabbing your arm on the street. It's cheap, unearned, and it repels.

**CLOSING CTA (bottom of page):** Emotional. Earned. Specific. By now the reader has scrolled through everything — the story, the proof, the characters, the details. They *know* this place or this person. The closing CTA is the payoff. It uses what the page already established to create a feeling the reader can't ignore.

**The rule:** The top CTA is a door. The bottom CTA is a destination. Never reverse them.

---

## THE EARNING MECHANISM

The closing CTA can only land if the page earns it. Here's what "earning" means:

The CTA is a **callback**, not a setup. It references things the reader already encountered on the page. If the page never introduced grandma, you can't invoke grandma. If the page never mentioned a 47-0 winning streak, you can't use "be there for 48."

Think of it like a punchline. The joke only works if the setup came first. A closing CTA that references something the page didn't build is a punchline to a joke nobody heard.

**How a page earns its CTA — the FalCaMel example:**

The page for a fictional Saudi cat café on a cliff (FalCaMel) follows this arc:

| Section | What it earns |
|---------|--------------|
| Hero: "Grandma's House. On the Edge." | Establishes the grandma metaphor |
| The Lounge: "Grandma's living room. 200 meters above everything you thought mattered." | Builds the world — you're inside her house now |
| The Residents: Sultan the falcon (47-0 vs. the rollercoaster), Haboob the camel (judges your outfit), Shams and Qamar the rescue cats | You've met the family |
| Our Majlis: "She'll make you feel like the only guest in the world." | Warmth deepened |
| What Grandma Made: luqaimat, kunafa, Jareen single-origin qahwa, mutabbaq. "You're not ordering. You're accepting what's offered." "Grandma-sized portions. Yes, all of it." | She's been cooking. You can smell it. |
| The Full FalCaMel: evening programs, SAR 2,000 experiences | The full picture |
| Find Us: "If you get lost, look up. We're on the edge." | You know where she lives |

**Then the footer lands:**

> "Grandma's house. 200 meters up. She made too much food again. She always does. Don't keep her waiting."

Every word in that CTA was earned by the page. "Too much food" — you saw the menu. "Don't keep her waiting" — you've been in her house for five minutes. The guilt is real because the generosity was real. The warmth is real because you met her residents and ate her food.

## THE BENCHMARK

Here are the hero (top) and closing (bottom) CTAs from five different vibes of the same café. Study the contrast:

| Vibe | Hero CTA (top — boring) | Closing CTA (bottom — earned) |
|------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Grandmother's House** | "Find Your Seat" | "Grandma's Not Getting Any Younger." / *She made luqaimat. She made kunafa. She's been cooking since you said you were coming. The coffee's already poured.* / **[I'm Coming, I'm Coming]** |
| **Qahwa Tales** | "Taste the Altitude" | *(narrative closes with the Royal FalCaMel — the complete story earns the booking)* |
| **The Race** | "Watch The Race" | "THE STREAK CONTINUES" / *47 races. 47 wins. Be there for 48.* / **[Witness the Streak]** |
| **The Antidote** | *(no hero CTA — the page just pulls you in)* | "Your seat is waiting." / *No rush. It will still be here.* / **[Find Your Seat]** |
| **Majlis** | "Claim Your Sanctuary" | "Your Throne Awaits" / *The Majlis opens its doors to precious few. Will you be among the chosen?* / **[Request Royal Audience]** |

Notice: every hero CTA could belong to any business. "Find Your Seat." "Taste the Altitude." Generic. Functional. That's correct — the top hasn't earned specificity yet.

Every closing CTA could only belong to *this* business. "I'm Coming, I'm Coming" only works after grandma. "Be there for 48" only works after you've met Sultan. "No rush. It will still be here." only works after a page that deliberately moved slowly. The closing CTA is the page's fingerprint.

## ANTI-PATTERNS: WHAT FAILS

**1. Generic FOMO**
"Only 3 spots left!" / "Limited availability — book now!"
This is fear, not feeling. It works on commodity products (hotel rooms, flights). It's death for anything with personality. It tells the reader you don't trust your own page to be compelling, so you're manufacturing urgency instead.

**2. Corporate action language**
"Schedule your consultation today" / "Get started" / "Learn more" / "Contact us"
This describes a button function. It tells the reader what happens when they click, which they already know. It creates zero feeling. It's the equivalent of a waiter saying "this is a fork" before you eat.

**3. Unearned emotion at the top**
"Your life is about to change!" / "Join the family!" / "This is where it all begins!"
Emotional language before the page has established anything. The reader hasn't met the family. They don't know what "it" is. This is the stranger grabbing your arm. It doesn't create warmth — it creates suspicion.

**4. Clever-for-clever's-sake**
"Don't just stand there. Sit down." / "You know you want to." / "What are you waiting for?"
These try to be witty but they're generic. They could go on any page for any business. A great closing CTA is specific to *this* page's content. If you can move the CTA to another site and it still works, it's not good enough.

**5. The Wikipedia hedge**
"If you're interested in learning more about our offerings, feel free to reach out at your convenience."
This is compliance-officer mode. It hedges every possible commitment. It's the opposite of a reflex — it's an invitation to think about it later, which means never.

## HOW TO BUILD A CLOSING CTA: THE PROCESS

1. **Write the full page first.** The CTA comes last — literally last in your workflow. You cannot write a closing CTA for a page that doesn't exist yet.

2. **List what the page earned.** What characters did the reader meet? What specific details did they absorb? What feelings did the page create — warmth, excitement, awe, trust, humor?

3. **Identify the gap.** What is the reader's situation right now (reading the page, considering but not committed) versus where the business takes them (at the table, in the room, on the call)? The CTA lives in that gap.

4. **Write the CTA as a callback.** Use the page's own language, characters, or imagery. The CTA should feel like the page's last line, not an add-on.

5. **Make the button text a human response, not an action label.** "I'm Coming, I'm Coming" is what you say to grandma. "Witness the Streak" is what you'd tell a friend. "Request Royal Audience" is how you'd describe it to someone. The button is dialogue, not interface.

6. **Test it: reflex or decision?** Read the full page, then read the CTA. Did you feel something, or did you evaluate something? If you evaluated, rewrite. A great CTA bypasses the rational brain.

## APPLYING THIS TO ANY BUSINESS

This isn't just for cafés. The principle scales:

**Personal/career site:** The page walks through credentials, outcomes, career arc. The closing CTA should make the hiring manager feel the cost of *not* reaching out — not describe what happens if they do. The gap: they have a complex cross-border deal going sideways and no one in the room who reads the situation. The CTA lives in that gap.

**SaaS product:** The page walks through features, case studies, integrations. The closing CTA should invoke the specific pain the reader came with — not "Start your free trial" but something that makes them feel the weight of continuing without the product.

**Event/experience:** The page walks through what happens, who's there, what it feels like. The closing CTA should make absence feel like loss — not "Register now" but something that makes not-going feel like a missed chapter.

**The universal test:** Can you move this CTA to a competitor's page and have it still work? If yes, it's generic. Kill it. A great closing CTA is load-bearing — it only stands because the page beneath it holds it up.

## SUMMARY FOR QUICK REFERENCE

| Position | Job | Emotion level | Specificity |
|----------|-----|--------------|-------------|
| Hero CTA (top) | Wayfinding | Zero — functional only | Generic is fine |
| Closing CTA (bottom) | Payoff | Maximum — earned by the page | Must be unique to this business and this page |

**The test:** Does the CTA describe an action, or trigger a reflex?

If it describes an action, it belongs at the top.
If it triggers a reflex, it belongs at the bottom.
If it does neither, delete it.
